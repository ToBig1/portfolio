01 Geeks for Social Change Feb 2026 - Present

Understanding How Community Organizations Adopt and Navigate Digital Coordination Tools

End-to-end UX research for PlaceCal, a community wealth-building platform, surfacing usability gaps and user needs to support its expansion from the UK to global contexts.

+

The Challenge

PlaceCal was built to help neighborhood organizations share local events and services, but adoption outside its original UK context was limited. The question was whether the platform's design assumptions held for community groups with different digital literacy levels, organizational structures, and coordination habits.

Research Process

  • 01
    Discovery and Contextual InquirySemi-structured interviews and contextual inquiry sessions with community organization coordinators to understand how they currently discover, evaluate, and adopt digital tools for local coordination.
  • 02
    Persona DevelopmentBuilt user personas grounded in research data to give the product and engineering team a shared, evidence-based picture of distinct community user groups and their diverging needs.
  • 03
    Journey MappingDeveloped user journey maps capturing the end-to-end experience of community organizers using PlaceCal, surfacing drop-off points, friction moments, and unmet needs across the adoption arc.
  • 04
    Participatory Design and Usability EvaluationFacilitated participatory design sessions with community users and conducted moderated usability testing sessions to evaluate task completion, identify pain points, and surface navigation issues.
  • 05
    Heuristic EvaluationConducted heuristic evaluations of the PlaceCal interface against established usability principles to surface accessibility and navigation issues ahead of user testing.
  • 06
    Synthesis and ReadoutSynthesized qualitative findings into research readouts and affinity maps, delivering actionable insights to cross-functional product and engineering stakeholders.

Key Findings

  • Community coordinators evaluated tools based on social trust, not feature lists. Adoption was more likely when a trusted peer had already used the platform.
  • Onboarding flows assumed organizational consistency that many grassroots groups did not have, creating early friction.
  • Journey mapping revealed a gap between initial interest and active use, particularly around the first event submission experience.
  • Heuristic evaluation surfaced navigation inconsistencies that compounded for users with lower digital literacy.

Impact

Research findings directly informed iterative platform improvements and roadmap prioritization for PlaceCal's global expansion strategy beyond the UK.

02 Independent Research Project 4 Months

Transforming Maternal Health Access: Asset Mapping for Hispanic Communities in Savannah, GA

A community-centered research and design project that reframed asset mapping as a conversation tool to help Hispanic mothers and local organizations surface and share bilingual lactation support services.

+

The Challenge

Hispanic mothers in Savannah and Chatham County, Georgia face compounding barriers to lactation support: language gaps, geographic mismatches, and fragmented community knowledge about available services. While resources exist, they are clustered in central Savannah while the highest-density Hispanic populations live in suburban areas like Garden City (18.6-21.6% Hispanic) and Port Wentworth (13.2%).

Initial research focused on cataloguing services, but demographic analysis revealed the real problem was not a lack of resources but a lack of shared language, access, and community infrastructure to make those resources findable and usable.

How Research Changed the Direction

The scope pivoted from static service listing to designing a toolkit for structured community conversations. Demographic data showing 65% Hispanic population growth in Chatham County since 2010 made it clear that a one-time map would quickly become outdated. Communities needed tools to keep having the conversation themselves.

Research and Design Process

  • 01
    DiscoveryQualitative document analysis and provider interviews to understand how organizations currently address language barriers. Key finding: most relied on telephone interpretation lines or patient-provided translators rather than dedicated bilingual staff.
  • 02
    AnalysisAffinity diagramming and demographic synthesis to identify themes. Three core gaps emerged: reliance on hospital-based translation, geographic mismatch between services and populations, and no shared vocabulary for discussing bilingual equity in maternal care.
  • 03
    DesignDeveloped a low-fidelity Asset Mapping Toolkit designed for low-tech environments, including community conversation cards, a defining-terms activity to build shared language, an onboarding booklet, and a QGIS-based live mapping guide.
  • 04
    Build and EvaluateBuilt a live geospatial map using QGIS. Evaluated toolkit effectiveness through exploratory analysis and incorporated updated 2024 demographic estimates to validate design decisions.

Key Findings

  • Asset mapping for underserved communities is a relational process, not a data exercise. Trust and shared language matter more than map accuracy.
  • Provider interviews revealed a gap between formal translation infrastructure and families' actual experience of language access.
  • Low-tech tools designed for conversation outperformed digital-first approaches in communities with variable internet access.
  • Geographic alignment between service locations and population density is a structural gap that requires advocacy, not just better maps.

Impact

The toolkit addresses needs for approximately 25,000-27,000 Hispanic residents (8.9% of Chatham County's 307,336 population). It provides community leaders and healthcare providers with tools to facilitate ongoing, equity-focused conversations about breastfeeding support access.

03 Bristol Myers Squibb, R&D Summer 2024 & Summer 2025

Patient-Centered Research to Inform Advocacy Operations and Platform Strategy

Generative and evaluative research with Patient Advocacy Groups to surface insights that shaped clinical study protocol design and a scalable operations playbook adopted across global R&D teams.

+

The Challenge

Bristol Myers Squibb's R&D division needed to better understand how Patient Advocacy Groups (PAGs) experienced the PEER protocol review process and how their insights were being integrated into clinical trial design. The advocacy engagement model had grown organically across teams but lacked a consistent, research-informed operational framework.

Research Approach

  • 01
    Generative ResearchDesigned and executed generative studies to understand patient and stakeholder needs across clinical development workflows, focusing on how PAGs currently participated in protocol review and where their input was absorbed or lost.
  • 02
    Evaluative ResearchConducted PEER protocol reviews with PAGs, evaluating the usability and clarity of the process from the patient advocacy perspective and identifying specific points where feedback was misaligned with how clinical teams received it.
  • 03
    Cross-functional Stakeholder InterviewsConducted structured interviews across R&D and Corporate Affairs teams to understand how research insights traveled through the organization and where they lost fidelity in translation.
  • 04
    Synthesis and Playbook DevelopmentSynthesized findings into the Advocacy Operations Playbook, a strategic framework for consistent, research-informed engagement across global patient engagement teams.

Key Findings

  • PAG feedback was often gathered but not systematically actioned, creating a trust gap between advocacy groups and the clinical teams they engaged with.
  • The PEER review process lacked a shared framework for what constituted an actionable design improvement, leading to inconsistent uptake across programs.
  • Advocacy engagement worked best when researchers had pre-established relationships with PAGs rather than project-by-project outreach.

Impact

The Advocacy Operations Playbook was adopted across global patient engagement teams within R&D. Research findings informed experience strategy changes across the R&D organization, presented directly to Corporate Affairs leadership.

04 Financial Start-up, Lagos (Freelance) Freelance

Designing a Compliant, User-Centered Onboarding Experience for a Nigerian Microfinance Platform

Freelance UX research to improve the onboarding flow of a financial services platform navigating Nigeria's Central Bank identification requirements while serving users with varied documentation access.

+

The Challenge

A financial start-up in Lagos needed to onboard new customers in a way that met Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) requirements for Microfinance banks, including BVN verification and identity documentation, while remaining accessible to users who may not have standard identification. The onboarding experience needed to balance compliance, usability, and trust.

Research Approach

  • 01
    User SegmentationAligned with internal and external stakeholders to segment users by their familiarity with the onboarding process and documentation access, identifying two core segments: internal users with process knowledge and external users experiencing the flow for the first time.
  • 02
    Feature PrioritizationRan a card sorting exercise with 6 participants, including 3 with prior onboarding experience, to surface what features users prioritized and why. Participants ranked features by importance to reveal gaps between the designed experience and user expectations.
  • 03
    Qualitative InterviewsPost-sorting interviews explored participant thoughts on the onboarding process, navigation difficulties, reasons behind feature prioritization choices, and suggestions for improvement.

Key Findings and Recommendations

  • Certain features were difficult to detect on desktop due to sizing, with discoverability dropping further on mobile.
  • Users recommended new quick-access links to streamline compliance steps and reduce cognitive load during identity verification.
  • Mobile usability required specific layout adjustments that the current desktop-first design had not accounted for.

Background

I am a UX Researcher and doctoral candidate in Public Health at Georgia Southern University. My work sits at the intersection of human-centered design, health equity, and community experience. Before coming to UX research through product organizations, I spent years conducting qualitative and mixed-methods research in academic, clinical, and community settings.

That background gives me something specific: I know how to research users whose experiences are shaped by systems they did not design and cannot easily opt out of. Whether that is patients navigating clinical trial processes, community organizations working with underfunded digital tools, or citizens trying to engage with political institutions that were not built for them, I know how to surface what is actually happening and why it matters.

I have worked with Bristol Myers Squibb, Geeks for Social Change, Denali Therapeutics, and community organizations across Georgia and Nigeria. I bring a researcher's rigor and a storyteller's instinct to every study I run.
